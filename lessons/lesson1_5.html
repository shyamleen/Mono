In a single morning, a busy modern company might sell a product to dozens of clients, accept raw material shipments accompanied by vendor payment terms, pay factory workers, and settle utility bills. To keep these events organized, accountants use a strict chronological system known as the General Journal.

The First Step: Validating Source Documentation

A professional accountant never entries values based on memory or hearsay. Every single entry into the journal must be backed by a verifiable source document—the concrete proof that a financial transaction occurred.

  • Invoices: Official forms indicating billing terms for sales made on credit.
  • Receipts: Immediate documentation verifying cash exchanges or digital payments.
  • Bank Statements: Verifiable institutional records of automated wire transfers, deposits, and fees.

The Standard Anatomy of a General Journal Entry

The General Journal functions as the daily log book of a business. To ensure clarity for global auditors, entries follow a strict structural formatting standard:

  • The Header Date: Listed first on the left column.
  • The Debit Account Line: Written flush against the left margin. The value is placed in the Left Debit numeric column.
  • The Credit Account Line: Placed beneath the debit line and indented slightly to the right. The value is placed in the Right Credit numeric column.
  • The Explanation: A short, italicized narrative note underneath providing context or referencing a specific source invoice.
