Lesson 1.4: The Journal and Ledger

A Journal Entry is used in double-entry bookkeeping to officially record a financial transaction. It is the business’s diary, recording transactions chronologically as they happen. In every single entry, total debits must equal total credits in at least two accounts.

Anatomy of a Journal Entry

Every standard journal entry requires five core components to be legally complete:

📅 1. Date The exact day the financial exchange took place.
📖 2. Accounts Affected Debits are written on top. Credits are indented below.
➕ 3. Debit Amount The numeric value recorded in the left-hand column.
➖ 4. Credit Amount The numeric value recorded in the right-hand column.
📝 5. Description: A brief, simple note added to the bottom explaining the business purpose of the transaction.

The General Ledger

After we have recorded transactions chronologically in the General Journal, we must sort them into separate accounts. This posting process is compiled in the General Ledger, tracking running balances for each individual account.

Steps to Post a Transaction

1
Identify the Accounts

Look at your journal entries to determine which specific accounts (e.g., Cash, Capital) are affected.

2
Record the Date

Enter the transaction date in the ledger account chronologically to preserve the audit trail.

3
Transfer Amounts

Put debit amounts on the left side and credit amounts on the right side of the ledger account.

4
Update Running Balance

Calculate the new running balance after every entry (De­bits/Cre­dits modify this dynamically).

The Trial Balance

The Trial balance is a report that lists the ending balances of every general account to verify that total debits equal total credits.

How to Summarize Ledger Balances

Summarizing your ledger accounts is the first step in creating a trial balance.

🔍 Find Ending Balances For each account in your general ledger, calculate the final balance by subtracting the smaller side from the larger side.
Example: If an asset has $5,000 in debits and $2,000 in credits, the ending balance is a $3,000 Debit.
🗂️ Sort by Type Organize the balances into your trial balance worksheet using the standard accounting order: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Revenues, and Expenses.
⚖️ Normal Balances
Debit Column: Assets, Expenses, and Dividends.
Credit Column: Liabilities, Equity, and Revenue.

How to Check Your Math

📊 Sum the Columns Sum the Columns: Add all values in the debit column to find the total debits. Repeat the process to find the total credits.
🔄 Compare Totals Compare Totals: The total sum of the debit column must exactly match the total sum of the credit column.
⚠️ Identify Discrepancies Identify Discrepancies: If the totals do not match, you have arithmetic or bookkeeping errors that must be investigated and resolved.
