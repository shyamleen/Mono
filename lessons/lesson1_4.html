The core challenge of accounting is mastering the structural logic of changing financial values. If a company acquires heavy manufacturing machinery, pays cash down for inventory, or clears a vendor debt, you must know exactly how to reflect that change across the ledger without breaking the accounting equation.

Deep-Dive Rules for the Core Accounts

The directional rules governing debits and credits align directly with where an account sits in the accounting equation ($Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity$).

1. Asset Accounts (Left Side of the Equation)
Because assets are on the left, their "normal" balance is a debit. To increase an asset, you record a debit. To decrease it, you record a credit.

2. Liability and Equity Accounts (Right Side of the Equation)
Because these accounts are on the right, their "normal" balance is a credit. To increase them, you record a credit. To decrease them, you record a debit.

The Expanded Framework: Revenues and Expenses

Owner's Equity changes over time through everyday trading operations. It increases when the business generates revenue and decreases when it incurs expenses. This gives us two final, critical tracking rules:

  • Revenues Increase Equity: Because equity increases with a credit, revenues are increased with a Credit entry.
  • Expenses Decrease Equity: Because expenses pull equity down, they do the opposite. Expenses are increased with a Debit entry.

Memory Shortcut: The DEALER System

To make memorizing these rules simple, accountants often divide the word DEALER into two clear functional groups:

  • D-E-A (Increased by Debit): Dividends, Expenses, and Assets.
  • L-E-R (Increased by Credit): Liabilities, Equity, and Revenue.
