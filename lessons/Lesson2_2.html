MODULE 2: FINANCIAL STATEMENTS

Lesson 2.2: The Income Statement

An income statement, or profit and loss (P&L) statement, is a core financial document that summarizes a company’s revenues, expenses, gains, and losses over a specific period. It begins with total revenue (the "top line") and ends with net income (the "bottom line") to show overall profitability.

How to Make an Income Statement

1. Set the Header

Every income statement requires a standard three-line header:

  • Company Name: [Company Name]
  • Statement Name: Income Statement (or Profit and Loss Statement)
  • Time Period: "For the month ended [Date]" or "For the year ended [Date]"

2. List Your Revenues

Start by tracking all the money your business earned from selling goods or services.

  • Gross Revenue / Sales: The total amount of money made before any deductions.
  • Total Revenue: Gross revenue minus returns, discounts, or refunds.

3. Account for Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

Subtract the direct costs required to produce or deliver your products/services. This includes raw materials, labor, and shipping.

Gross Profit = Total Revenue - COGS

4. Deduct Operating Expenses

List your indirect, day-to-day business costs, such as rent, utilities, marketing, and employee salaries.

Operating Income = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses

5. Factor in Interest and Taxes

Account for expenses not directly related to day-to-day operations, such as interest on business loans and corporate income tax.

Net Income = Operating Income - Interest and Taxes

Key Terms to Remember

  • Revenue / Sales: The "top line" represents total income from selling goods or providing services before any expenses are deducted.
  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): The direct costs associated with creating the product or delivering the service, such as raw materials and direct labor.
  • Gross Profit: Revenue minus COGS. This indicates how efficiently a company uses labor and supplies in the production process.
  • Operating Expenses (OpEx): Day-to-day overhead costs required to run the business that aren't tied directly to production, such as rent, salaries, marketing, and utilities.
  • Operating Income: Gross profit minus operating expenses. This measures the profit earned from regular, core business operations.
  • Non-Operating Items: Revenues, expenses, gains, or losses that fall outside regular business operations, such as interest paid on debt, interest earned on investments, or taxes.
  • Net Income: The "bottom line." This is the final profit or loss after all revenues, expenses, and taxes have been accounted for.
