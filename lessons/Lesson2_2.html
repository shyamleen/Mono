The Income Statement (P&L)

An income statement, or profit and loss (P&L) statement, is a core financial document that summarizes a company’s revenues, expenses, gains, and losses over a specific period. It begins with total revenue (the "top line") and ends with net income (the "bottom line") to show overall profitability.

🛠️ How to Make an Income Statement

1. Set the Header

Every income statement requires a standard three-line header:

Company Name: [Company Name]
Statement Name: Income Statement (or Profit and Loss Statement)
Time Period: "For the month ended [Date]" or "For the year ended [Date]"

2. List Your Revenues

Start by tracking all the money your business earned from selling goods or services.

  • Gross Revenue / Sales: The total amount of money made before any deductions.
  • Total Revenue: Gross revenue minus returns, discounts, or refunds.

3. Account for Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

Subtract the direct costs required to produce or deliver your products/services (materials, direct labor, shipping).

Gross Profit = Total Revenue - COGS

4. Deduct Operating Expenses

List your indirect, day-to-day business costs, such as rent, utilities, marketing, and salaries.

Operating Income = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses

5. Factor in Interest and Taxes

Account for expenses not directly related to day-to-day operations, such as loan interest and corporate taxes.

Net Income = Operating Income - Interest and Taxes

Revenue / Sales

The "top line" represents total income from selling goods or providing services before any expenses are deducted.

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

The direct costs associated with creating the product or delivering the service, such as raw materials, direct shipping, and factory labor.

Gross Profit

Calculated as Revenue minus COGS. This metric reveals how efficiently a business handles its primary materials and production labor during assembly.

Operating Expenses (OpEx)

Day-to-day overhead costs required to run the business that aren't tied directly to physical production, such as commercial rent, corporate salaries, utilities, and marketing.

Operating Income

Calculated as Gross profit minus Operating Expenses. This critical benchmark tracks the underlying cash efficiency of core normal operations.

Non-Operating Items

Revenues, expenses, gains, or losses that fall outside regular business activities, such as interest paid on corporate loans, tax payments, or gains from sold investments.

Net Income

The "bottom line." This is the final absolute profit or loss generated after every category of revenue, cost, expense, non-operating charge, and tax is subtracted.
