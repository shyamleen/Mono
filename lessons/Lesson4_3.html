Accounts Receivable & Bad Debt

Accounts receivable (AR) represent the money customers owe your business for credit sales. Bad debt occurs when those invoices become uncollectible, usually due to customer bankruptcy or refusal to pay. Under accrual accounting, businesses estimate and record bad debt as an expense to avoid overstating their assets.

The Allowance Method (GAAP Required)

To ensure financial statements remain accurate, businesses primarily rely on the allowance method (required by GAAP) to anticipate and record bad debt. This forward-looking process estimates bad debt and matches it to the period of the related sale, reporting accounts receivable at their net realizable value and preventing major swings in reported profits when specific invoices are written off.

  • Bad Debt Expense: An income statement expense account that records the estimated amount of uncollectible invoices matching the revenue generation window.
  • Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (AFDA / ADA): A contra-asset account on the balance sheet that reduces the total gross Accounts Receivable to what you realistically expect to collect.
  • Write-Offs: When an account becomes definitively uncollectible, you remove it from your books. Debit the AFDA account and credit the specific customer’s AR. This does not impact net income because the estimated expense was already recorded.
