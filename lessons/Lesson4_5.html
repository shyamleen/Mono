Shareholders' Equity

Lesson 4.5

Corporate equity represents the ownership interest in a company, calculated as total assets minus total liabilities. The core components of shareholders' equity include equity stocks (capital invested by shareholders) and retained earnings (cumulative net income kept in the business), while dividends are the portion of profits distributed to investors.

  • Equity Stocks: These represent ownership shares in the corporation. Common stock gives investors voting rights and a residual claim on assets, whereas preferred stock typically lacks voting rights but provides fixed dividends and priority during liquidation.
  • Dividends: These are cash or stock distributions paid to shareholders as a reward for their investment, which reduces the company's retained earnings. Dividend policies are set by the board of directors and signal the financial maturity of the business.
  • Retained Equity (Retained Earnings): This is the cumulative portion of a company’s net income that is reinvested into the business rather than paid out to stockholders. These funds are used to pay off debt, fund research and development (R&D), or expand operations, ultimately increasing the company's long-term value.
