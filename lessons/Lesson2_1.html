Introduction to Financial Statements

Financial statements are formal records that summarize a company’s financial positions over a specific period of time, such as a quarter or a year.

⚖️

Balance Sheet

Statement 1 of 4

Provides a snapshot of a company's financial position at a single, specific point in time. It outlines what the business owns (Assets) and what it owes (Liabilities), with the remainder representing Shareholders' Equity.

Total Liabilities $30,000
Shareholders' Equity
Common Stock & Retained Earnings $120,000
Total Liabilities & Equity $150,000

Income Statement

Statement 2 of 4

Also known as a Profit and Loss (P&L) statement, it details a company's revenues and expenses over a period. It calculates the final Net Income to show whether the business generated a profit or loss.

Accrual vs. Cash Basis Accounting

The core difference between the two accounting methods is timing: cash basis records transactions only when money physically changes hands, while accrual basis records revenue when it is earned and expenses when they are incurred.

💵 Cash Basis Accounting

Strictly tied to your actual bank account balance.

  • Revenue: Recorded when you physically receive payment (e.g., when a check clears or cash is in hand).
  • Expenses: Recorded when you actually pay a bill or vendor.
  • Best for: Freelancers, sole proprietors, and small service-based businesses with no inventory.
🟢 Pros: Simple to manage; gives a real-time picture of your actual liquidity.
🔴 Cons: Can create a misleading picture of profitability. For example, if you complete a $10,000 job in December but don't get paid until January, your December books will show $0 in income.

📅 Accrual Accounting

Matches revenues with the exact time period they were earned.

  • Revenue: Recorded when the service is completed or product is delivered. You use Accounts Receivable to track money clients owe you.
  • Expenses: Recorded when you receive the goods or services, using Accounts Payable to track bills you owe.
  • Best for: Growing businesses, companies holding physical inventory, or businesses seeking investors/loans.
🟢 Pros: Aligns with GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles); accurately shows how much money your business makes over a specific quarter or year.
🔴 Cons: More complex and resource-intensive; can be risky because your books might show a healthy profit even if your bank account is temporarily empty.
Key Differences at a Glance
Feature Cash Basis Accrual Basis
Income Recorded When cash is received When it is earned
Expenses Recorded When cash is paid When expenses are incurred
Financial Picture Shows cash on hand Shows overall profitability
Compliance Does not meet GAAP Meets GAAP requirements
